What I Built, What Broke, and What I'd Do Differently

// On building EcoTwin at Hack4impact

The Problem

Picture this: it's monsoon season in Delhi. The air quality is absolutely abysmal. People are posting health updates like weather forecasts. And here I am, a 4th year CS student thinking — "what if we could actually predict this stuff instead of just complaining about it?"

So I decided to build EcoTwin at Hack4impact. Spoiler alert: biting off more than you can chew in a 24-hour hackathon is a rite of passage.

What Actually Happened

The goal was simple-ish: grab live air quality data from NASA and OpenAQ, train an ML model to forecast AQI for the next 24 hours, and visualize pollution hotspots on a map. On paper, genius. In practice? Chaos wrapped in Python.

The first breaking point — the APIs weren't returning data the way I expected. Classic rookie mistake of assuming documentation matches reality. Spent 3 hours debugging what turned out to be a single missing field in the JSON response. Could've screamed. Didn't. (Barely.)

The second breaking point — XGBoost was overfitting like crazy. The model was 99% accurate on training data, 40% accurate on real data. Turns out feeding it 6 months of Delhi pollution data and expecting it to generalize forever doesn't work. Who knew? (Everyone. Everyone knew.)

The third breaking point — deploying FastAPI on a limited hackathon environment while also building the frontend and UI was like juggling while riding a unicycle. I crashed. Multiple times. But somehow it worked on demo day.

What I'd Change

If I could go back, I'd do three things differently:

  1. Start with bad data, not perfect data. Spend less time on the model, more time making sure the pipeline is bulletproof. A model that's 70% accurate but actually works beats 95% accurate code that breaks in production.
  2. Make the UI first. Seriously. I built the dashboard last and realized halfway through that the insights I was showing didn't make sense. Knowing what you're visualizing before you build it saves hours.
  3. Stop trying to be clever. I overengineered half the features. Sometimes a simple heatmap beats fancy predictions. Simple > impressive.

The Real Lesson

EcoTwin taught me that building something real is messier than tutorials make it seem. But that mess is where you actually learn. The bugs, the 2 AM debugging sessions, the "wait this should work why doesn't it work" moments — that's the actual education.

Also learned that competitive programmers aren't the only ones who need dashboards. People actually want to know if their neighborhood is gonna be unbreathable tomorrow.

Would I do it again? Yeah. Would I yell at my code less? Probably not.
